I'm afraid that I spent most of the month of July dealing with the same data corruption problem that I was fighting before THATCamp. This was unexpected, as I was pretty sure I'd solved the problem by changing hosts. In fact, I had solved that problem, but THATCamp highlighted a very similar one.

After THATCamp, a couple of people posted links to my beta. (Thanks to Jeanne Kramer-Smythe and Lisa Spiro!) A side effect of this publicity is that search engines started crawling my beta site. Now when a spider crawls through a site, it clicks on every link on the pages it's indexing. There's nothing wrong with this, so long as those links merely load new pages.

However, what if your links make changes? What if — as a short-cut — you fired an action that changes your data from a hyperlink, rather than constructing some sort of form with a submit button? Even the basic Rails book warns against this folly, but that doesn't mean seasoned programmers like me think it applies to us.

To make a long story short, my code for combinig subjects with other subjects used a hyperlink interface. It is fortunate for me that clicking this resulted in an easily-correctable error. Since Google, MSN, and other robots clicked every "combine this entry" link they saw, the corrupted data I knew how to fix was far better than combining each subject in a text with every other subject.
